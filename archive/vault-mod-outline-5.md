# Vault 23 — Concept Outline

## 1. General lore & premise

**Vault number**: Vault 23 — 23 pairs of human chromosomes, a quiet resonance for players who catch it, invisible to those who don't. Slots naturally into the Mojave's existing vault numbering alongside canon Vaults 22 and 24.

**Core premise (the experiment)**: Vault-Tec's experiment was to seal a single family in the vault, with the patriarch as Overseer, and have them interbreed and repopulate over generations. 200 years later, the vault opens due to a crisis — population is large, but riddled with genetic defects (founder effect / inbreeding depression).

**Inspirations / tone references**:
- Habsburgs — dynastic intermarriage for power preservation, physical degeneration as a consequence of a deliberate choice, not an accident. Maps directly onto the patriarch's self-justification for keeping the bloodline "pure."
- Mad Max War Boys — treated as a *separate, compounding* aesthetic layer (pallor, ritual, founder-worship from generations without real sunlight), not as the cause of the genetic issues. Two distinct vault horrors: isolation + inbreeding.

**The twist (revealed mid/late)**: the current generation recognized the genetic decline and tried to fix it by cracking open the vault's reactor and irradiating fetuses in the womb, hoping radiation induces beneficial mutation/diversity (Fallout-style pseudo-science, consistent with FEV/ghouls/mutants logic — not real-world biology). This requires a high birth rate to run enough trials, which is why all childbearing women in the vault are pregnant — the first thing the player notices, before understanding why. Side effect: radiation leakage is worsening and threatens the whole vault; repeated direct exposure during treatment is also making the mothers themselves sick.

**The cover story**: ~20 years ago, the Overseer publicly introduced "prenatal care" as a new initiative to reduce the vault's already-high infant mortality rate. Official story, told openly (including to the player): the prenatal care hasn't worked — mortality has gotten worse since it started — which is why so many women are pregnant at once (repeated attempts to compensate). The Overseer can say this sincerely and with real frustration; every word is true from the family's perspective and a lie about its actual cause. Reality: "prenatal care" is the delivery mechanism for radiation injections into the fetus, administered by the doctor. Plausible deniability comes from baseline dysfunction — the vault already had generations of stillbirths/deformities from ordinary inbreeding depression before this began, so the program's damage hides inside a decline the family already expected. Only a small circle knows the real mechanism: the Overseer, the doctor, and the Overseer's wife. The wife's knowledge is also why she stopped having children after her son — she knew what she was actually agreeing to, got a healthy result once, and chose not to risk it again.

**Succession mechanism**: fixed rule of eldest son of the eldest son, generation after generation. This means the son isn't fighting for a birthright he might lose — he already has it by the vault's own rule. What makes him a usurper is timing: he's taking power from a living father rather than waiting for natural succession.

## 2. Characters

**The Overseer (father) — George IX**: family patriarch, adamant against outside contact/diversity — sees the radiation program as the vault's own internal "family" solution, outsiders as contamination of the bloodline. Deliberately *not* written as denial or self-delusion — the program has real successes, some children genuinely are born healthier with more diversity, and it demonstrably works some of the time. Central moral question: sometimes it works — does that justify the deaths and suffering incurred getting there? His resistance to outsiders is also legitimate, not paranoid — the wasteland is genuinely dangerous, and it's a real, justified risk, not just purity rhetoric. He's balancing a known, contained cost he can control against an unknown, potentially existential one.

**The son (usurper/heir apparent) — George X**: opposes his father, believes outside genetic diversity is the fix. His view isn't a prior proposal that got overruled — it's simpler: the genetic decline is obvious, and his father isn't doing anything real about it, as far as he knows. He doesn't know about the radiation program, and doesn't know he himself is a product of it (born healthy, early in the program) — that ignorance is the dramatic irony at the center of his character, resolved later in a confrontation where he learns the truth. Currently an isolated dissenter, likely without organized allies, frustrated by inaction he can see but can't explain.

Naming convention: eldest son of the eldest son has always been named George, matching the fixed succession rule — royalty-style regnal numbering rather than personal names. ~200 years and 9 generations puts the current Overseer at George IX and the heir at George X, roughly a 22-year average generation gap, in line with historical dynastic succession.

**The Overseer's wife (mother) — Rebecca**: knows the truth, part of the small circle. Stopped having children after her healthy son — a knowing choice, not coincidence. Currently aligned with the father's position, but persuadable — the clearest swing vote in the story. Can be convinced to side with the son, or to broker a halt to the program without full regime change. Keeps her own given name rather than being folded into the numbered-George convention — a small but pointed contrast to how the men's identities collapse into sameness.

**The doctor — Silas**: a true believer, Mengele-archetype. Not conflicted, not coerced — he genuinely enjoys the experimentation for its own sake. No guilt, no internal arc needed; his coldness is the disturbing part. Serves as a moral fixed point with zero ambiguity, in contrast to the Overseer. Name choice leans into an old-fashioned, puritanical Anglo-American register (matching George/Rebecca) rather than a German name — despite the Mengele archetype, he's part of the same single bloodline as everyone else in the vault, so the "one heritage" constraint rules out foreign-coded naming; the coldness comes through in characterization, not nomenclature.

**Clyde**: the "hero" the vault sent out before the player arrived. Sent to find reactor parts needed to reseal the leak, with no real idea what he's looking for or how to find it — rants to the player about the impossible, humiliating position he was put in. Not brave, not competent. Reached Vault 21, became infatuated with Sarah, and — out of fear of the wasteland and social cowardice — never left and never approached her. Found seated in Vault 21's dining room, a talkable NPC fixture requiring no quest marker.

**Player-facing NPCs for trust-building**: the doctor, whoever runs the armory, someone in the kitchen — see Story Beats §3.2.

## 3. Story beats (chronological)

### 3.1 Discovery — Vault 21
- Courier encounters Clyde in Vault 21's dining room.
- Clyde is under instructions not to reveal what he was sent to find (reactor parts), but lets it slip. Player can call him on it directly with a **Perception check**.
- Player can convince Clyde they can help instead, via a high enough **Repair, Science, or Energy Weapons** check.
- If convinced, Clyde either uploads the vault's access key to the player's Pip-Boy, or agrees to escort them back himself. Alternative: player can kill him and take the access key by force.
- Since Sarah is a romanceable NPC, the player can tell Clyde they've already slept with her — angers/hurts him, but can be turned into leverage to get him to finally leave (she's taken, no reason to keep waiting around). Whether or not he ends up escorting, having his key does **not** soften the vault's suspicion of the player later — same trust-building phase regardless of how the player got in.
- Regardless of route, the player ends up with vault access and opens/enters Vault 23 themselves. Vault marked on map; Clyde reveals nothing about radiation or babies at this point — surface level only ("my vault needs help").

### 3.2 Arrival — earning trust inside Vault 23
- Vault is highly suspicious of an uninvited outsider walking in, regardless of how the player got the key.
- Trust is built by helping individual vault members with tasks that specifically require an outsider — access to the wasteland, skills, or supplies the vault itself lacks.
- Candidate NPCs: the doctor, whoever runs the armory, someone in the kitchen. Each is a vehicle for characterization/worldbuilding. The doctor's task in particular is a strong candidate for planting the first "something's off" thread without giving anything away yet.
- Open: exact content of each task still to be designed; kitchen/armory tasks likely lean toward characterization/worldbuilding, doctor's should carry more narrative weight.

### 3.3 Meeting the Overseer & introducing the son
- Once trust is earned, the Overseer calls the player in and asks for help with the reactor, revealing it's leaking.
- The son is introduced here: when the player walks in, the Overseer and son are already mid-argument over whether to open the vault to outsiders — live conflict rather than exposition.
- Overseer asks the player to help with the reactor regardless of the argument's outcome in the moment. Likely next step: the son escorts the player to the reactor, giving a one-on-one window to start building that relationship.

### 3.4 Investigation with the son
- **Everything from here through §3.6 is optional.** The player can instead just repair the reactor, collect payment from the Overseer, and leave — the errand resolves cleanly as a simple fetch/repair quest for a player who doesn't engage further. Investigating is something the player chooses to pursue, not something forced on them.
- On entering the reactor room, the son immediately says "Something is wrong here..." — an unprompted line, not gated behind talking to him.
- Talking to him gets him to voice it further: something feels off about the leak, and he wants to investigate further. He doesn't have the standing or expertise to act on it alone.
- **Skill check (Perception/Science/Repair)**: high enough, and the player identifies immediately that the reactor's condition isn't ordinary wear — it's been deliberately tampered with.
- If the check fails, there's no hard gate: the son instead suggests looking around the vault itself for clues, since neither of them can make sense of the reactor alone.
- Player walks through the family's individual housing units. Entering one that has radiation triggers a quest update ("radiation discovered in [resident]'s housing unit") and prompts the player to check the other units.
- Player checks the remaining units; more of them register radiation they shouldn't have. Same discovery either way, read differently depending on what the player already knows:
  - **Passed the reactor check**: the player already knows the reactor was tampered with, so finding radiation across the housing units is *confirming where the tampering is actually being used*.
  - **Failed the reactor check**: the player doesn't yet know it's deliberate, so the readings just raise an unexplained, unsettling pattern — the "why" lands later.
- This keeps the beat load-bearing for every build: no player is blocked from progressing the investigation, but skill investment changes what the player understands in the moment, not just whether they get to move forward.
- Once enough units are checked, the player returns to the son, found in the vault's eating space — he's visibly alarmed by the pattern. This conversation leads directly into the ghoul incident (§3.5).
- Player can gather proof of the real mechanism behind "prenatal care," which will eventually be what turns the son from passive dissenter into someone willing to act — and what eventually forces him to learn his own origin.

### 3.5 The ghoul incident (escalation, not inciting incident)
- Triggers directly out of the housing-unit findings in §3.4: while the son is still alarmed, reacting to the pattern with the player in the eating space, the incident breaks out.
- A pregnant woman, previously introduced to the player, complains of feeling unwell during/after a prenatal visit, then suddenly ghoulifies (green flash). She attacks nearby vault dwellers, killing one or two, before vault enforcers put her down.
- No one in the vault has ever seen a ghoul before — only the Courier recognizes what happened, repositioning the player from outsider-being-shown-around to the only person who understands the real stakes.
- Player gets a dialogue choice: name what they saw aloud (goes public fast) or stay quiet (private leverage to use later).
- The doctor removes the body "to run tests" — reads as normal to the family, reads as evidence-control/cover-up in hindsight. Narrative hook for wanting access to his clinic/lab.
- Aftermath: the family needs a fast, comforting explanation (bad luck, a curse, "she was always sickly") — quiet character work for the Overseer's founder-mythology language.
- Staging: likely a forced-encounter beat (locked room, scripted NPC combat trigger) rather than a true non-interactive cutscene.
- Open thread: who actually puts her down (Overseer, doctor, or son) — each implies a different character beat, especially if it's the son's radicalizing moment.
- Open thread (unresolved as of this revision): is the woman already in the eating space when she ghoulifies, right there with the player and George X, or does it happen elsewhere and the two of them rush toward it?

### 3.6 Confrontation and resolution
- Investigation culminates in a confrontation where the son learns the truth, including his own origin as an early success of the program.
- Player's accumulated evidence/relationships determine which ending path is available/taken (see Endings below).

**Exit mechanic**: under normal circumstances, the player can only leave the vault by talking to the Overseer — he's the one who provides the exit passcode. If the player kills him instead, they can loot the passcode off his body, but this turns the rest of the vault hostile toward the player. If the player leaves anyway after that, a voice comes over the intercom just outside the vault door, announcing that the vault is locking itself and will never open again. See Endings below for how this plays into the violence outcomes.

## 4. Endings

No single "good" ending — each path is a real trade-off, in keeping with NV's approach (NCR/Legion/House/Independent all costly in different ways). Skill investment changes *how* an ending is reached and what's learned along the way, not whether it's a clean win. Everything collapses into two root outcomes — the Overseer keeps power, or the son takes it — plus two separate, distinct root branches.

### A. Side with the father (Overseer keeps power)
0. **Walk away** — player skips the optional investigation entirely (§3.4): repairs the reactor, collects payment, and leaves without ever learning the truth. Not a choice about the secret — the player never has one to keep. Outcome looks identical to A1 from the outside, but for a different reason: no complicity, no radiation program in the picture at all as far as the player knows.
1. **Keep the secret** — program continues unchanged. Requires an active decisive moment (e.g., the son directly asks the player what they know; staying silent is a real act, not passivity). Resealing the reactor may directly sustain the horror going forward — the original "hero" errand becomes complicit rather than heroic.
2. **Mother-brokered halt** — player persuades the mother the program is wrong, and she in turn persuades the Overseer to halt the trials. Father remains in power, program ends. Doesn't fix the underlying genetic crisis or open the vault, but ends the child deaths and spread of sickness — an unsatisfying-but-humane middle path.

### B. Side with the son (usurper takes power, vault opens to outsiders)
1. **Kill the father** — cleanest regime change, no ambiguity about who's in charge, but requires the player to be comfortable with patricide-by-proxy and doesn't erase the son's own compromised origin.
2. **Forced exile** — depose without killing. Requires more than combat: enough of the family's support turned, the mother's backing, or leverage from the ghoul incident. Open question: can the father survive alone in the wastes afterward.
3. **Father steps down voluntarily** — highest-investment path (Speech/Science/Medicine/accumulated proof), where the player makes his position untenable rather than defeating him outright.

Explicitly rejected: an "expose everything to the vault population and let it erupt into chaos/mob justice" branch.

### C. True evil ending (separate root branch)
The Courier rigs the reactor to explode outright. No sub-variants — single, final outcome: nothing left of the vault but a hole in the ground. Gating still to be determined.

### D. Violence outcomes (separate from A/B/C)
Player-driven killing sits outside the political resolutions above — it isn't a vote in the succession conflict, it's opting out of that conflict entirely. See the exit mechanic in §3.6: leaving normally requires talking to the Overseer for the exit passcode.

1. **Kill everyone in the vault** — no succession, no negotiation, nothing left to resolve. The vault lies dormant, never to be opened again.
2. **Kill the Overseer, then leave** — distinct from B1: B1 kills the father specifically to install the son as the new Overseer; this is killing him with no resolution of who leads next. The player loots the exit passcode off his body instead of receiving it, which turns the rest of the vault hostile. Leaving afterward triggers the intercom warning that the vault is locking itself permanently.

**Flagged**: still need actual epilogue content for both "player kills X" outcomes above, and to decide whether a partial massacre (killing some family members short of everyone) is its own distinct branch or folds into one of these two.

## 5. Post-credits epilogue (NV-style slideshow)

- **A0 (walk away)**: Courier fixes the reactor, is paid, and leaves — same beats as A1, but with no mention of radiation, tampering, or a program at all, because the Courier never found out. Reads only as ordinary attrition: the unhealthy children died, the healthy ones lived. No moral weight is assigned, because none was ever revealed.
- **A1 (keep the secret)**: Courier says nothing, fixes the reactor, is paid, and leaves. Program continues in secret — many die, but eventually enough diversity is achieved to eradicate the genetic sickness and establish a healthy lineage. Courier never learns (or never reveals) whether it was worth it.
- **A2 (mother-brokered halt)**: father stops the trials, but the underlying genetic sickness was never cured. Vault continues to decline; residents eventually die out. The few healthy children produced before the halt leave the vault and walk the wastes.
- **B1 (killing the father)**: Overseer's death rattles the vault; the family comes to view the new Overseer as illegitimate. They overthrow him and attempt to reseal the vault, but are slaughtered by outsiders who won't accept the vault's permanent closure. *(Rough draft, logic/staging unresolved.)*
- **B2 (forced exile)**: father wanders the wastes until he dies of exposure. Son opens the vault; it's raided by the Legion, who capture and enslave many and kill the rest.
- **B3 (voluntary step-down)**: father steps down and stays on as an advisor to his son. Son opens the vault; it's raided by the Legion, who capture and enslave many and kill the rest.
- Note: B2 and B3 converge on the same Legion-raid outcome regardless of succession method — the vault's real vulnerability was opening the door at all. Thematic echo of the father's original, legitimate fear.
- **Flagged for rework**: the B epilogues currently read as too uniformly harsh — every "side with the son" path ends in disaster regardless of player buildup, undercutting the value of skill/investment within that branch. Tabled for now; needs revisiting so outcomes are more differentiated and at least one path is genuinely survivable.
- **D1 (kill everyone)**: no succession, no outsiders, no story left to tell. The vault lies dormant, never to be opened again.
- **D2 (kill the Overseer, then leave)**: *(not yet written)* — needs its own epilogue distinct from B1's, since no successor is installed and the vault is left leaderless and hostile rather than transitioning to the son.
