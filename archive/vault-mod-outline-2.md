# Vault [TBD] — Concept Outline

## Core premise (the experiment)
- Vault-Tec's experiment: seal a **single family** in the vault, with the patriarch as Overseer.
- The family is expected to interbreed and repopulate the vault over generations.
- 200 years later, the vault opens due to a crisis — population is large, but riddled with genetic defects (founder effect / inbreeding depression).

## Inspirations / tone references
- Habsburgs — dynastic intermarriage for power preservation, physical degeneration as a consequence of a deliberate choice, not an accident. Maps directly onto the patriarch's self-justification for keeping the bloodline "pure."
- Mad Max War Boys — treated as a *separate, compounding* aesthetic layer (pallor, ritual, founder-worship from generations without real sunlight), not as the cause of the genetic issues. Two distinct vault horrors: isolation + inbreeding.

## The twist (revealed mid/late)
- The current generation recognized the genetic decline.
- Their "fix": crack open the vault's reactor and irradiate fetuses in the womb (delivered via the vault doctor during "prenatal care"), hoping radiation induces beneficial mutation / diversity (Fallout-style pseudo-science, consistent with FEV/ghouls/mutants logic — not real-world biology).
- This requires a high birth rate to run enough trials → **all childbearing women in the vault are pregnant**. This is the first thing the player notices, before understanding why.
- Side effect: radiation leakage is worsening and now threatens the whole vault; also, repeated direct exposure during treatment is making the mothers themselves sick. They need outside expertise to reseal the reactor. (See "cover story" section below for delivery mechanism and secrecy.)

## Structure: FO1 homage
- Vault sends out a "hero" — a healthy adult male generalist — to find someone capable of resealing/fixing the reactor.
- He is not brave and not especially competent. He reaches **Vault 21**, becomes infatuated with Sarah, and — out of fear of the wasteland and social cowardice — never leaves and never approaches her.
- Courier finds him seated in Vault 21's dining room (no active quest marker needed to discover him — a talkable NPC fixture there).
- He explains the situation at a surface level only: his vault needs a scientist/mechanic/capable generalist. No mention of radiation or babies — that's discovered on-site.
- He gives the vault's location (no need to make finding it a challenge) — this simply marks the vault on the map.
- He does not accompany the Courier back by default (fear-driven refusal). Optional beat: the player can persuade/escort him back to the vault as a followable NPC; his reaction on arrival is scripted and depends on whatever ending state has been reached by that point.
- Since Sarah is a romanceable NPC, add a dedicated dialogue option where the player can tell him they've already slept with her — this angers/hurts him, but can also be turned into leverage to get him to finally leave: she's taken, she's not going to end up with him, so there's no reason left to keep waiting around Vault 21. Funny inversion — the thing that should make him shut down becomes the thing that talks him out the door.

## The cover story (how known is the program?)
- The cover is simple and requires no separate ritual or vocabulary: ~20 years ago, the Overseer publicly introduced "prenatal care" as a new initiative to reduce the vault's already-high infant mortality rate.
- Official story, told openly (including to the player): the prenatal care hasn't worked — mortality has actually gotten worse since it started — which is why so many women are pregnant at once (repeated attempts to compensate). The Overseer can say this sincerely and with real frustration; every word of it is true from the family's perspective and a lie about its actual cause.
- Reality: "prenatal care" is the delivery mechanism for radiation injections into the fetus, administered by the doctor. The worsening mortality isn't the genetic decline continuing on its own — it's the radiation actively making a bad problem worse (except in the cases where it doesn't, and produces a genuinely healthier, more diverse child).
- Plausible deniability comes from baseline dysfunction: the vault already had generations of stillbirths/deformities from ordinary inbreeding depression before this began, so the program's damage hides inside a decline the family already expected and has a ready explanation for.
- Only a small circle knows the real mechanism: the **Overseer**, the **doctor**, and the **Overseer's wife**. The rest of the vault, including initially the usurper, believes the official "failed medical program" story. The wife's knowledge is also *why* she stopped having children after her son — she knew what she was actually agreeing to, got a healthy result once, and chose not to risk it again.

## Succession mechanism
- Fixed rule: **eldest son of the eldest son**, generation after generation. Avoids the ambiguity of a sprawling single-bloodline family tree and removes "who's legitimate" as an open question.
- This reframes the conflict: the son isn't fighting for a birthright he might lose — he already has it by the vault's own rule. What makes him a **usurper** is timing: he's taking power from a living father rather than waiting for natural succession. He's right about the diagnosis (outsider diversity), but the method (deposing his own father) is still morally loaded.

## Late-story set piece: the ghoul incident
- Placed near the **end** of the story, after all major players (Overseer, usurper, doctor) have been introduced — an escalation/climax beat, not the inciting incident.
- A pregnant woman, previously introduced to the player, complains of feeling unwell during/after a prenatal visit, then suddenly ghoulifies (green flash). She attacks nearby vault dwellers, killing one or two, before vault enforcers put her down.
- No one in the vault has ever seen a ghoul before — **only the Courier recognizes what happened**, which repositions the player from outsider-being-shown-around to the only person who understands the real stakes.
- Player gets a dialogue choice on whether to name what they saw aloud (goes public fast) or stay quiet (private leverage to use with the son/doctor/Overseer later).
- The doctor removes the body "to run tests" — reads as normal to the family, reads as evidence-control/cover-up in hindsight once the player knows more. Also a narrative hook for the player to want access to his clinic/lab.
- Aftermath: the family needs a fast, comforting explanation for the deaths (bad luck, a curse, "she was always sickly") — a good moment for the Overseer's founder-mythology language to do quiet character work without him needing to be overtly villainous.
- Staging note: likely handled like other NV forced-encounter beats (locked room, scripted NPC combat trigger) rather than a true non-interactive cutscene, given engine limitations.
- Open thread: who actually puts her down (Overseer, doctor, or the usurper) — each implies a different character beat, especially if it's the usurper's radicalizing moment.

## Key character psychology
- **The doctor**: a true believer, Mengele-archetype. Not conflicted, not coerced — he genuinely enjoys the experimentation for its own sake. No guilt, no internal arc needed; his coldness is the disturbing part. Serves as a moral fixed point with zero ambiguity, in contrast to the Overseer.
- **The Overseer**: deliberately *not* written as denial or self-delusion. The program has real successes — some children genuinely are born healthier, with more genetic diversity, and the radiation approach demonstrably works some of the time. This is the central moral question the player has to sit with: sometimes it works — does that justify the deaths and suffering incurred getting there?
  - His resistance to outsiders is also **legitimate, not paranoid**: the wasteland is genuinely dangerous, and letting outsiders into the vault is a real, justified risk to the family's safety, not just bloodline purity talk. He's balancing a known, contained cost he can control (his own family's suffering) against an unknown, potentially existential one (destabilization or violence from outside contact).
  - Net effect: both the Overseer and the usurper are arguing from real, defensible positions. The player/usurper aren't opposing a madman — they're opposing a working (if monstrous) system with a legitimate justification behind its caution.

## Ending branches (resolved structure)
No single "good" ending — each path is a real trade-off, in keeping with NV's approach (NCR/Legion/House/Independent all costly in different ways). Skill investment changes *how* an ending is reached and what's learned along the way, not whether it's a clean win. Everything ultimately collapses into two root outcomes — the Overseer keeps power, or the usurper takes it — each with distinct sub-variants underneath.

### A. Side with the father (Overseer keeps power)
1. **Keep the secret** — program continues unchanged. Requires an active decisive moment (e.g., the son directly asks the player what they know; staying silent is a real act, not passivity). Resealing the reactor (the original quest) may directly sustain the horror going forward — the "hero" errand becomes complicit rather than heroic.
2. **Mother-brokered halt** — the player persuades the mother the program is wrong, and she in turn persuades the Overseer to halt the trials. Father remains in power, but the program ends. Doesn't fix the underlying genetic crisis, doesn't open the vault to outsiders, but ends the child deaths and the spread of sickness among the mothers — an unsatisfying-but-humane middle path within this branch.

### B. Side with the son (usurper takes power, vault opens to outsiders)
1. **Kill the father** — cleanest regime change, no ambiguity about who's in charge, but requires the player to be comfortable with patricide-by-proxy and doesn't erase the son's own compromised origin (born of the same program he's now dismantling).
2. **Forced exile** — depose without killing. Requires more than combat: enough of the family's support turned, or the mother's backing, or leverage from the ghoul incident. Open question: can the father actually survive alone in the wastes afterward — could be left ambiguous, or the player could later encounter the consequence.
3. **Father steps down voluntarily** — the highest-investment path (Speech/Science/Medicine/accumulated proof), where the player makes his position untenable rather than defeating him outright. Regime changes to the usurper without violence or forced removal.

Explicitly rejected: an "expose everything to the vault population and let it erupt into chaos/mob justice" branch — decided against, at least for now.

### C. True evil ending (separate root branch)
The Courier rigs the reactor to explode outright. No sub-variants — single, final outcome: nothing left of the vault but a hole in the ground. Gating (skill check, dialogue path, or accessibility conditions) still to be determined.

## Post-credits epilogue (NV-style slideshow)
Following New Vegas's convention of a post-credits slideshow describing how choices played out over subsequent years:

- **A1 (keep the secret path)**: the Courier says nothing, fixes the reactor, is paid, and leaves. The program continues in secret — many die, but eventually enough diversity is achieved to eradicate the genetic sickness and establish a healthy lineage. The Courier never learns (or never reveals) whether it was worth it.
- **A2 (mother-brokered halt path)**: the father stops the trials, but the underlying genetic sickness was never cured. The vault continues to decline; residents eventually die out. The few healthy children produced by the experiments before the halt leave the vault and walk the wastes.
- **B1 (tied specifically to killing the father)**: the Overseer's death rattles the vault; the family comes to view the new Overseer as illegitimate. They overthrow him and attempt to reseal the vault, but are slaughtered by the outsiders who won't accept the vault's permanent closure. (Rough draft — logic and staging still need work.)
- **B2 (tied to forced exile)**: the father wanders the wastes until he dies of exposure. The son opens the vault; it's raided by the Legion, who capture and enslave many and kill the rest.
- **B3 (tied to voluntary step-down)**: the father steps down and stays on as an advisor to his son. The son opens the vault; it's raided by the Legion, who capture and enslave many and kill the rest.
- Note: B2 and B3 converge on the same Legion-raid outcome regardless of how the succession happened — the vault's real vulnerability was opening the door at all, not the manner of succession. A thematic echo of the father's original, legitimate fear about outsiders.

## Vault power dynamics
- Entire population self-identifies as **one family**.
- **Current Overseer** = family patriarch. Adamant against outside contact/diversity — sees the radiation program as the vault's own internal, "family" solution; outsiders = contamination of the bloodline.
- **Heir apparent** (the son) opposes him — believes outside genetic diversity is the fix. His view isn't a prior proposal that got overruled; it's simpler than that: the genetic decline is obvious to anyone looking, and his father isn't doing anything real about it (as far as he knows — he doesn't know about the radiation program, and doesn't know he himself is a product of it). That ignorance is the dramatic irony at the center of his character, resolved later in a confrontation where he learns the truth.
- Succession mechanic: fixed **eldest-son-of-eldest-son** rule (see Succession mechanism section) — the son already holds his position by right, which is what makes deposing a *living* father an act of usurpation rather than a contested claim.
- Son's current position: isolated dissenter, likely without organized allies, frustrated by inaction he can see but can't explain, and unaware of the deeper truth beneath it.
- Player's role: can investigate and bring the son proof, turning him from a passive dissenter into someone willing to actually move against his father — making the Courier the actual instigator of the coup, not just a bystander picking a side. This proof is also what eventually forces him to learn his own origin.
- One resolution path: overthrow/depose the current Overseer, install the son, open the vault to outside genetic diversity as the long-term fix (see Ending branches for the full range of outcomes).
