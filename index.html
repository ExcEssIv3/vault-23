FILE 01

Premise & Lore

General background, the twist, and the cover story the family tells itself.

Vault number 23 — a quiet nod to 23 pairs of human chromosomes, slotting naturally beside canon Vaults 22 and 24.

The experiment. Vault-Tec sealed a single family in the vault, patriarch as Overseer, to interbreed and repopulate over generations. 200 years later the vault opens due to a crisis: population is large, but riddled with genetic defects from founder-effect inbreeding.

Tone references. The Habsburgs — dynastic intermarriage for power, degeneration as the result of a deliberate choice, not an accident. Mad Max's War Boys, treated as a separate, compounding aesthetic layer (pallor, ritual, founder-worship) rather than the cause of the genetics. Two distinct vault horrors, stacked: isolation, and inbreeding.

The twist. The current generation recognized the decline and tried to fix it by cracking the reactor and irradiating fetuses in the womb, chasing beneficial mutation. This demands a high birth rate to run enough trials — why every childbearing woman in the vault is pregnant, the first thing the player notices, long before they understand why. Side effect: the leak is worsening, and repeated exposure is making the mothers sick too.

The cover story. Twenty years ago the Overseer publicly introduced "prenatal care" to fight infant mortality. Every word of the official story is true from the family's perspective and a lie about its actual cause — plausible deniability comes from a baseline of dysfunction the vault already had. Only the Overseer, the doctor, and his wife know the real mechanism.

Succession. Fixed rule: eldest son of the eldest son. The son isn't fighting for a birthright he might lose — he already has it. What makes him a usurper is timing: taking power from a living father instead of waiting.

FILE 02

Cast of Characters

Personnel on record inside Vault 23, plus one straggler in Vault 21.

George IX — The Overseer

Father · Patriarch

Adamant against outside contact. Sees the radiation program as the family's own internal fix, outsiders as contamination. Not written as denial: the program has real successes. Central question — does sometimes-working justify the deaths incurred getting there? His fear of outsiders is legitimate, not just purity rhetoric.

George X — The Son

Heir Apparent · Usurper

Believes outside genetic diversity is the fix, without knowing about the radiation program — or that he himself is one of its early, healthy successes. Dramatic irony resolved later in confrontation. Currently an isolated dissenter, frustrated by inaction he can see but can't explain.

Rebecca — The Wife

Mother · Swing Vote

Knows the truth. Stopped having children after her one healthy son — a knowing choice. Currently aligned with the father, but persuadable: can be turned to the son's side, or brokered into halting the program without regime change. Keeps her given name, unlike the numbered Georges.

Silas — The Doctor

Physician · True Believer

Mengele-archetype, genuinely enjoys the experimentation for its own sake. No guilt, no arc needed — the coldness is the disturbing part. A moral fixed point against the Overseer's ambiguity. Trust-building task: a mundane RadAway/RadX resupply, plus an optional, shadier ask for irradiated ghoul brain matter "for testing" — a deniable first hint of his real nature.

Clyde — The Failed Hero

Vault 23 Envoy, Absent

Sent to find reactor parts with no idea what he was looking for. Reached Vault 21, fell for Sarah, and — out of fear and cowardice — never left and never approached her. Found seated in Vault 21's dining room, no quest marker required.

Phil — The Armorer

Gun Nut

Building an anti-materiel rifle off a schematic found in the vault library, at a bench that also holds a Mr. Handy he's been scrapping for parts. Trust-building task: fetch build components (jury-riggable with a high Repair check), plus an optional side goal teaching him to shoot it.

Haley — The Cook

Lead Chef

Runs the kitchen with a couple of dwellers under her. The family's sick of the same generations-old rations — trust-building task is fetching wasteland fruit, meat, and spice to vary the menu, optionally rounded out with a Crimson Caravan cookbook or a high Survival check to teach her directly.

Teresa — The Assistant

Silas's Assistant

Suspicious of Silas but unwilling to act on suspicion alone. A high Speech check convinces her outright; failing it sends the player after physical proof instead — a tissue sample cut from the incinerated ghoul's body in the vault freezer. Either way, she hands over the password to Silas's computer. Introduced right after the ghoul incident, when Silas stonewalls the player about the "autopsy."

FILE 03

The Vault Itself

Layout, life support, and who actually lives here.

Layout. Three levels, Vault-Tec ring-and-spoke.

  • Level 1 — Entrance & Atrium. The vault door leads directly into the Overseer's office and quarters, not the atrium — arrivals are received on the Overseer's terms before they ever see the vault's public heart. The atrium itself runs both stories open-air, with a catwalk crossing it at the upper level; the Overseer's office sits even with that upper level, with a window looking down into the atrium. The armory, a bar/celebration room (this vault's answer to Vault 101's cake room), and a janitor's closet all open off the atrium at ground level.
  • Level 2 — Family & Common Areas. Individual housing units, one per household; the dining hall, site of the ghoul incident; the kitchen; the library/archive, where Phil found his rifle schematic and where a shelf of hand-written family journals sits; and a classroom.
  • Level 3 — Medical & Reactor. Silas's clinic and his locked office, the incubator/prenatal ward, the freezer, water treatment, and the reactor room itself — the site of the deliberate tampering.

Life support. Power, water, and air all trace back to the same aging reactor — the leak isn't an isolated problem, it's the first visible crack in a system that's been quietly running down for 200 years. Food is the open question: no hydroponics has ever been established, so the kitchen runs on generations-old stored rations — the actual root of Haley's trust-building request.

Resident roster. Built bottom-up from working roles and marriages, rather than a top-down population guess.

  • Ruling family: George IX (Overseer), George X (heir).
  • Enforcement: Walter, Edgar, Thomas.
  • Medical: Silas, Teresa.
  • Kitchen: Haley (lead), Elias, Constance.
  • Armory: Phil.
  • Other working roles: Amos (maintenance), Prudence (teacher), Caleb (water treatment), Agnes (janitor).
  • Elderly: Mabel, widowed, unofficial keeper of the family journals.

Families. George IX & Rebecca (parents of George X); Walter & Teresa (Josiah); Edgar & Haley (Eleanor, Nathaniel, Clara, and Simeon); Thomas & Agnes (Beatrice, Gideon, Harriet, and Reuben); Elias & Constance (Ida and Jonas); Amos & Prudence (Martha, Tobias, and Norah). 18 named adults and 14 named children so far, plus unnamed background dwellers still to be layered on.

FILE 04

Story Beats

Chronological path through the quest, 3.1 through 3.7.

3.1Discovery — Vault 21

  • Courier meets Clyde, who lets slip his real mission (a Perception check catches him outright).
  • Convince him via Repair / Science / Energy Weapons, or take his access key by force.
  • Either way, the player ends up with vault access and enters Vault 23 — Clyde reveals nothing about radiation or babies yet.

3.2Arrival — Earning Trust

  • The vault is suspicious of any uninvited outsider, regardless of how the player got in.
  • Trust built via three tasks, each with a main goal and an optional side goal for extra reward:
  • Phil (armory): fetch scrap metal, scrap electronics, and a fission battery to finish his rifle build — jury-riggable with a high Repair check into just scrap metal, duct tape, and Wonderglue. Side goal: teach him to shoot it (Milsurp Review magazines, or a high Guns check), rewarded in ammo.
  • Haley (kitchen): fetch mutfruit, banana yucca, bighorner meat, brahmin meat, jalapeno pepper, and honey mesquite pod. Side goal: a Crimson Caravan cookbook or a high Survival check to teach her the recipes directly.
  • Silas (doctor): a RadAway/RadX resupply. Side goal, shadier: 5 samples of irradiated ghoul brain matter "for testing," rewarded with Stimpaks — a first hint of his real nature, well before anyone in the vault has even seen a ghoul.

3.3Meeting the Overseer

  • Trust earned, the Overseer calls the player in to help with the leaking reactor.
  • The son is introduced mid-argument with his father over opening the vault to outsiders.
  • The son likely escorts the player to the reactor — a one-on-one window to build the relationship.

3.4Investigation with the SonOptional path

  • The player can simply repair the reactor, get paid, and leave — investigating is a choice, not a gate.
  • The son immediately senses something's wrong. A Perception / Science / Repair check reveals deliberate tampering; failing it instead sends the pair searching the vault's housing units.
  • Checking units flags radiation readings across the family's quarters — read differently depending on whether the reactor check passed.
  • Returning to the son (found in the eating space) leads straight into the ghoul incident.

3.5The Ghoul Incident

  • The player and George X watch from an adjacent room, through a window into the eating area, rather than being in the room themselves.
  • A pregnant woman, unprompted, suddenly ghoulifies in the eating area, kills one or two dwellers, and is put down by vault officers.
  • Only the Courier recognizes what happened — the vault has never seen a ghoul before.
  • George X tells the player to wait — he doesn't think an outsider stepping in will help — and leaves. Fade to black.
  • On return, he explains Silas has already removed the body "for an autopsy," and asks the player directly if they know what happened — the player can tell him it was a ghoulification, or stay quiet.

3.6Silas's Office & Teresa

  • The player confronts Silas about the "autopsy" directly — he's evasive, no information gained.
  • Introduces Teresa, his assistant, who's suspicious of him. A high Speech check convinces her outright; failing it, she wants proof of her own first.
  • Proof means breaking into the vault freezer, where the ghoulified woman's body awaits incineration. A Medicine check lets the player carefully cut a small sample; too low, and they have to butcher the body and take the whole hand instead, via the gore system.
  • Teresa examines the sample and recognizes radiation-driven cellular transformation far beyond anything natural — either path ends with her handing over the password to Silas's computer, which opens his safe.
  • His office door radiates on approach even without that information, so no player is hard-blocked from noticing something's wrong.
  • Inside: RadX/RadAway stock in the office; irradiated material, a syringe, experiment notes, and a note from the Overseer himself in the safe — proof the Overseer is directly involved, not just turning a blind eye.
  • Quest updates: "Bring the proof to George X" (critical path) and an optional "Bring the proof to the Overseer" — who, if visited first, explains everything and asks the player to keep quiet, without closing off telling George X afterward.
  • With the proof in hand, two quiet resolutions remain available without ever confronting anyone: walk away (reactor repaired, reward collected, proof unused), or bring it to Rebecca and let her broker a halt with the Overseer directly.

3.7Confrontation & Resolution

  • Bringing the proof to George X triggers a meeting in the Overseer's office — no longer avoidable once started.
  • The player walks in mid-argument: the son says the program is wrong and can't work; the Overseer insists "It will work!" — X asks how he knows — "Because that's how you were born!" The son learns his own origin on the spot.
  • The Overseer refuses to end the trials or open the vault to outsiders.
  • Afterward, the player can still pursue the mother or side-with-father resolutions, or now back George X in overthrowing his father — this is where the B-branch endings become available.
  • Exit: normally requires talking the Overseer out of the passcode. Killing him lets the player loot it instead, but turns the vault hostile and seals the vault forever once the player leaves.
FILE 05

Endings

No clean win — every path is a real trade-off, NV-style. Two root outcomes, plus two separate branches.

A · Side With the Father
A0 — Walk Away

Skip the investigation entirely: fix the reactor, get paid, leave. Not a choice about the secret — the player never learns there is one.

A1 — Keep the Secret

The program continues. An active, decisive silence — the original "hero" errand becomes complicit rather than heroic.

A2 — Mother-Brokered Halt

Persuade Rebecca, who persuades the Overseer to stop the trials. Doesn't fix the genetic crisis, but ends the child deaths.

B · Side With the Son
B1 — Kill the Father

Cleanest regime change, but requires comfort with patricide-by-proxy and doesn't erase the son's compromised origin.

B2 — Forced Exile

Depose without killing — needs turned family support, the mother's backing, or ghoul-incident leverage.

B3 — Voluntary Step-Down

Highest-investment path: make the father's position untenable rather than defeating him outright.

C · True Evil
Single outcome

The Courier rigs the reactor to explode outright. No sub-variants: nothing left of the vault but a hole in the ground.

D · Violence Outcomes
D1 — Kill Everyone

No succession, no negotiation. The vault lies dormant, never to be opened again.

D2 — Kill the Overseer, Then Leave

Distinct from B1: no successor installed. Looting the passcode turns the vault hostile; leaving triggers the intercom lockout warning.

FILE 06

Epilogues

Post-credits slideshow, one entry per ending path.

A0 — Reactor fixed, paid, gone. Reads as ordinary attrition; no moral weight, because none was ever revealed.
A1 — Program continues in secret. Many die, but eventually enough diversity is achieved to end the sickness. The Courier never learns whether it was worth it.
A2 — Trials stop, but the underlying sickness was never cured. The vault declines and dies out; the few healthy children leave to walk the wastes.
B1 — The father's death delegitimizes the new Overseer. The family overthrows him and tries to reseal the vault, but is slaughtered by outsiders who won't accept a permanent closure. (rough draft, logic unresolved)
B2 / B3 — Converge on the same outcome: the vault opens and is raided by the Legion, who enslave many and kill the rest — the father's original fear, vindicated either way. Flagged for rework
D1 — No succession, no outsiders, no story left to tell. The vault lies dormant forever.
D2 — Not yet written — needs an epilogue distinct from B1, since no successor is installed and the vault is left leaderless and hostile.
